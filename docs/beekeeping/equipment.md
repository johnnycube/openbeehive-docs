# Equipment & hive types

Beekeeping does not demand a great deal of kit, but a few good tools make every visit calmer, safer and quicker. This page covers the essentials, then gives an overview of the main hive systems so you can choose the one that suits you and record it correctly in Openbeehive.

:::tip
When you create a hive in Openbeehive, you pick its type (Zander, Dadant, Deutsch Normal, Langstroth, Warre, Top-bar or Other). Choosing the right one keeps your frame counts and equipment notes consistent across your apiary. See [Hives](/using-the-app/hives).
:::

## The essentials

### The hive itself

A modern movable-frame hive is built from stacked boxes. The lowest is the **brood box**, where the queen lays and the colony rears young. Above it sit **supers**: shallower (or sometimes equal-sized) boxes for surplus honey.

Inside each box hang **frames**, usually with a sheet of **foundation** — a thin wax (or plastic) sheet embossed with the hexagonal cell pattern. Bees draw this out into comb. Some beekeepers prefer **foundationless** frames and let the bees build natural comb instead.

A few more parts complete the stack:

| Part | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Floor / bottom board | The base, with an entrance; often a mesh floor for ventilation and varroa monitoring |
| Queen excluder | A grid that keeps the larger queen out of the supers, so honey stays brood-free |
| Crown board / inner cover | Sits under the roof; gives bee space and a feed hole |
| Roof | Weatherproof top, often telescoping or gabled |

### Protective clothing

Comfort matters more than bravado. A **veil** to protect your face and eyes is non-negotiable; most beginners use a full suit or smock with an integrated veil. Add **gloves** (leather or nitrile) and tuck trousers into boots or use ankle straps.

Light-coloured, smooth fabrics provoke fewer bees than dark, fuzzy ones. As you gain confidence you may wear less, but never skimp on eye protection.

### Smoker

The **smoker** is your single most useful calming tool. Cool, gentle smoke masks alarm pheromones and prompts bees to move down into the comb, making the colony easier to handle.

Light it well before you open a hive and keep it producing cool, white smoke — never hot or flaming. Good fuels include dry grass, wood shavings, hessian or pine needles.

:::caution
Always extinguish your smoker fully and store it on a non-flammable surface. Smokers are a common cause of accidental fires near apiaries, especially in dry weather.
:::

### Hive tool

A flat steel **hive tool** is used constantly — to prise apart boxes glued with propolis, lever up frames, and scrape away wax and brace comb. Keep a spare; they are easy to set down and lose in the grass.

### Feeder

A **feeder** lets you give the colony sugar syrup (in spring or autumn) or fondant (in winter). Common types include contact (bucket) feeders, frame feeders that hang inside the box, and tray feeders that sit above the crown board.

Record feeding in Openbeehive so you know what each colony received and when. See [Feeding](/beekeeping/feeding).

### Extraction basics

To take honey off, you will need a way to uncap the sealed cells (an uncapping fork or heated knife) and a means of removing the honey from the comb. Most beekeepers use a **centrifugal extractor**, which spins honey out of the frames so the drawn comb can be reused. You will also want a strainer and clean food-grade buckets for settling and bottling.

For a full walkthrough, see [Honey harvest](/beekeeping/honey-harvest) and record yields under [Harvests](/using-the-app/harvests).

## Hive systems

The hive types differ mainly in box and frame dimensions, which determine how much comb a box holds and whether parts are interchangeable between systems. **Pick one system and stick to it** — mixing dimensions across colonies quickly becomes a frustrating muddle.

| Type | Origin / region | Frame style | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Langstroth** | USA; widely used worldwide | Movable frame | The global standard; vast choice of compatible equipment |
| **Dadant** | France/USA | Movable frame, deep brood | Large brood box favoured by some commercial beekeepers |
| **Zander** | Germany | Movable frame | Common in parts of Germany and central Europe |
| **Deutsch Normal** | Germany | Movable frame | A traditional German standard size |
| **Warré** | France | Top-bar, stacked | "Vertical top-bar"; minimal intervention, no full frames |
| **Top-bar** | Various | Horizontal top-bar | Single long box; bars instead of frames, natural comb |

Movable-frame systems (Langstroth, Dadant, Zander, Deutsch Normal) all work in the same broad way — vertical stacks of boxes with full rectangular frames — and differ mostly in dimensions. **Top-bar** and **Warré** hives take a lower-intervention approach: bees build comb from simple bars, which means gentler management but no honey extractor (comb is usually crushed and strained).

If your hive does not match any of these, choose **Other** in Openbeehive and note the details in the hive's description.

:::note
There is no single "best" hive. The most practical choice is usually whatever your **local association and nearby beekeepers** use, so you can borrow equipment, swap frames and get hands-on help. For a deeper comparison and frame dimensions, see [Hive types](/knowledge-base/hive-types).
:::

## Buying new vs used

New equipment is clean, predictable and arrives flat-packed or assembled — but it costs more. Used kit is cheaper and often perfectly good, especially boxes, roofs and tools.

The serious risk with second-hand gear is **disease**. Old comb and frames can harbour spores of **American foulbrood (AFB)**, which survive for decades and are not destroyed by ordinary cleaning. Buying used drawn comb or frames is the classic way to import a devastating, notifiable disease into your apiary.

:::danger
Never buy or accept used **comb, frames with comb, or honey supers** unless you are certain of their disease history. AFB spores can remain viable for 40+ years. When in doubt, do not.
:::

Sensible guidance for used equipment:

- **Boxes, floors, roofs, hive tools, smokers** — generally safe. Scorch wooden parts with a blow torch or scrub with a soda solution before use.
- **Frames and comb** — avoid unless from a trusted source with a clean bill of health. Old comb is cheap to replace and best renewed regularly anyway.
- **Whole colonies** — only buy from a reputable beekeeper, ideally after a frame-by-frame health inspection. Check the brood pattern and look for signs of disease.

Disease reporting and rules vary by country and region; **AFB is notifiable in many places**. Check your national bee health authority and consult your local association before introducing any used equipment or new colonies. See [Diseases & pests](/knowledge-base/diseases-and-pests).
