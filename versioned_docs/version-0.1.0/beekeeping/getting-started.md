# Getting started with beekeeping

Welcome. Keeping bees is one of the most rewarding things you can do with a small corner of a garden, a rooftop or an allotment. It connects you to the seasons, rewards patience, and (in a good year) fills a few jars with honey you grew yourself.

This page is a friendly orientation for absolute beginners. It will not turn you into a beekeeper on its own, but it will help you understand what you are taking on and how to start responsibly. Openbeehive is here to keep your records once your bees arrive, so you can spend more time at the hive and less time scribbling in a damp notebook.

## What keeping bees actually involves

Honey bees are not pets. A colony is a living superorganism of tens of thousands of individuals that mostly looks after itself. Your job is to provide good housing, keep an eye on the colony's health, manage its natural urge to swarm, protect it from pests and disease, and harvest any surplus the bees can spare.

In practice that means:

- **Regular inspections** through the active season (roughly spring to late summer), opening the hive to check the colony is healthy, has room, and has a laying queen.
- **Pest and disease management**, above all monitoring and treating for the varroa mite.
- **Swarm management** in late spring and early summer, when colonies naturally try to reproduce by splitting.
- **Feeding** when nectar is scarce, and ensuring the colony has enough stores for winter.
- **Record-keeping**, so you remember what each colony is doing and can spot trends over time.

:::tip
Good records are one of the biggest favours you can do your future self. Note what you saw at each visit, what you did, and what you plan to do next. Openbeehive's [Inspections](/using-the-app/inspections) feature is built around exactly this rhythm.
:::

## Time and cost expectations

Be honest with yourself about both before you commit.

**Time.** During the busy season you should expect to inspect each colony roughly every 7 to 10 days, with a single colony taking around 20 to 40 minutes once you are confident. There is quieter work in autumn and winter (feeding, mite treatment, equipment repair, reading), but the colony is largely dormant. Many hobbyists keep one to three hives comfortably alongside a full-time job.

**Cost.** Start-up costs vary widely by region and by whether you buy new, second-hand or build your own. As a rough guide, expect to budget for:

| Item | Notes |
| --- | --- |
| A hive | New, second-hand, or self-built. Choose a [hive type](/knowledge-base/hive-types) common in your area. |
| Protective clothing | A suit or jacket plus gloves. |
| Basic tools | Hive tool, smoker, bee brush. |
| Bees | A nucleus colony ("nuc") or a swarm. |
| Sundries | Feed, frames, foundation, varroa treatment. |

:::caution
Second-hand equipment can save money but can also carry disease, especially spores that survive for years. Only buy used woodwork from a trusted source, and clean and sterilise it thoroughly before use.
:::

## Join a local association and find a mentor

This is the single most valuable step a beginner can take, and it usually costs very little.

Local beekeeping associations run beginners' courses, often have an apiary where you can handle bees under supervision, and put you in touch with experienced keepers. Many also include third-party insurance and disease-support schemes in their membership.

A **mentor** is worth more than any book or video. Bees behave differently from region to region and season to season, and a local mentor can explain what you are actually seeing in your own hive. Ask your association to pair you with someone nearby.

:::note
Try to handle bees at a club apiary, and ideally complete a beginners' course, *before* you buy your own colony. A season of supervised experience will save you a great deal of money and worry.
:::

## Registering your apiary (varies by country)

Many countries require or strongly encourage beekeepers to register their hives so that authorities can trace and contain notifiable diseases. Rules differ enormously:

- In some countries registration is a **legal requirement**; in others it is voluntary but encouraged.
- There may be rules about hive spacing, fencing, water provision, or distance from public paths and neighbours' boundaries.
- Selling honey is usually subject to **food-labelling and hygiene regulations**.

:::danger
Always check the rules that apply where you live before keeping bees. Your local association or national beekeeping body is the best source, and registration is often free. Failing to register can hamper disease control for everyone.
:::

## Choosing your first apiary site

You do not need much space, but a good site makes everything easier. Look for:

- **Sunlight and shelter.** A spot that catches morning sun and is sheltered from strong, prevailing wind.
- **A dry, level base.** Avoid frost pockets and damp hollows.
- **A flight path away from people.** Face entrances towards a hedge or fence so bees rise quickly above head height, away from paths, doorways and neighbours.
- **Water nearby.** Bees need water; provide a safe source if there is none, so they do not visit a neighbour's pond or paddling pool.
- **Reasonable access.** You will carry heavy supers of honey, so think about the walk from car to hive.

When you are ready, you can record the location, hives and colonies in the app. See [Apiaries](/using-the-app/apiaries) and [Hives](/using-the-app/hives) to get set up.

## A realistic first-year plan

Every region's calendar differs, so treat this as a shape rather than fixed dates. See the [Beekeeping year](/beekeeping/beekeeping-year) for a fuller month-by-month guide.

1. **Winter before you start.** Join your association, take a beginners' course, read, and order equipment so it is ready in spring.
2. **Spring.** Acquire your first colony, ideally a local nucleus from a reputable supplier. Install it, feed if needed, and begin gentle weekly inspections.
3. **Late spring to early summer.** Watch closely for swarming. Give the colony room and learn to read queen cells. This is the steepest part of the learning curve.
4. **Summer.** If the colony is strong and the flow is good, you may take a small honey harvest, but do not expect much in the first year.
5. **Late summer.** Treat for varroa as soon as the honey is off, while there is still time for healthy winter bees to be reared.
6. **Autumn.** Feed the colony up to its winter weight and reduce the entrance against robbing and mice.
7. **Winter.** Leave the bees in peace. Heft the hive to gauge stores, keep the entrance clear, and plan for next year.

:::tip
Aim to keep your colony alive and healthy through its first winter. That, not honey, is the real measure of a successful first year.
:::
