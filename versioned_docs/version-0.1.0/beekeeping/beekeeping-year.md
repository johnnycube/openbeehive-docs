# The beekeeping year

Beekeeping follows the seasons. The colony grows, peaks, contracts and rests in a yearly rhythm driven by daylight, temperature and the flowers in bloom. Learning that rhythm is the heart of the craft: your job is mostly to read what the bees are doing and stay one step ahead.

This page is a season-by-season guide. Treat the months as a rough map, not a timetable, and adjust everything to your own conditions.

:::note Hemisphere and climate vary
Calendar months below assume the Northern Hemisphere. In the Southern Hemisphere, shift everything by roughly six months (spring is September to November). Either way, your local climate matters more than the date: a warm coastal site runs weeks ahead of a cold upland one. Watch the bees and the forage, not the calendar.
:::

## Late winter to early spring: build-up

As days lengthen, the queen starts laying again and the colony begins to grow. This is a delicate time. The cluster is rebuilding its numbers but forage is scarce and the weather is unreliable.

What to do:

- Keep disturbance to a minimum. A quick "heft" of the hive (lifting one side to judge weight) tells you whether stores are running low without opening up.
- On the first mild, still day, a brief peek confirms the colony is alive, queenright and has food. Feed if stores are short (see [Feeding](/beekeeping/feeding)).
- Clean and prepare equipment: spare boxes, frames and foundation for the season ahead.
- Plan your varroa strategy now rather than in a panic later (see [Varroa](/beekeeping/varroa)).

Record the first inspection and any feeding as you go. Logging stores levels early gives you a baseline for the year.

## Spring: swarm season

This is the busiest and most rewarding stretch. Colonies expand fast, the first strong nectar flows arrive, and reproduction kicks in. Swarming is the colony's natural way of multiplying, and spring is when it happens.

What to do:

- Inspect roughly every seven to nine days through the build-up. This cadence matches the queen-cell cycle and lets you catch swarm preparations before they go too far.
- Watch for queen cells, congestion and a colony "boiling" with bees. Give space early by adding boxes or supers.
- Apply your chosen swarm-control method if cells appear (see [Swarming](/beekeeping/swarming)).
- Consider raising new queens or making increase from strong stock (see [Queen rearing](/beekeeping/queen-rearing)).

:::tip Use a weekly task to stay on rhythm
The seven-day inspection cycle is easy to lose track of across several hives. Create a recurring inspection task per apiary so the next visit is always on your list. See [Tasks](/using-the-app/tasks).
:::

## Summer: the honey flow

In high summer the main nectar flow is on. Colonies are at full strength and, if the weather cooperates, filling supers quickly.

What to do:

- Keep ahead of the bees with super space. A crowded colony stores less and is more likely to swarm.
- Continue inspections, but you can ease the frequency once the swarming urge passes (often after the longest day).
- Monitor varroa levels through the season so you are not surprised at harvest time.
- Harvest capped honey when frames are ready and the flow tails off (see [Honey harvest](/beekeeping/honey-harvest)).

Record each harvest with weights and which hives produced it. Over a few seasons this turns into genuinely useful data about your best colonies and your best forage.

## Late summer: treat and feed

This is arguably the most important moment of the whole year. The bees that will carry the colony through winter are reared now, and they must be healthy. That means getting varroa under control as soon as the honey is off.

What to do:

- Remove honey supers, then assess and treat for varroa promptly. Late treatment lets mites damage the winter bees.
- Use an approved treatment for your region and follow the label exactly.
- Once treatment allows, feed to top up winter stores if the colony is light. Feed concentrated syrup in good time so the bees can ripen and cap it before it turns cold.

:::caution Treatment rules vary by country
Approved varroa treatments, dose rates and timing differ between regions, and some have legal requirements. Always use a product licensed where you live and follow national guidance. See [Varroa](/beekeeping/varroa) for an overview, but check local rules.
:::

Log every treatment: product, dose and dates. This protects you, helps you judge what works, and keeps an honest health history for each hive. The app's [Treatments](/using-the-app/treatments) records are designed exactly for this.

## Autumn: preparation

The colony is contracting and preparing to overwinter. Your aim is a strong, healthy, well-provisioned colony in a sound, weatherproof hive.

What to do:

- Confirm stores are sufficient for your winter. Heft the hives and feed if still light.
- Check the colony is queenright with a good laying pattern going into winter.
- Reduce entrances to help the bees defend against robbing and mice; fit mouse guards.
- Ensure ventilation and that the hive sheds rain. Secure roofs against wind.
- Tidy and store drawn comb safely against wax moth.

Use tasks to make sure no hive is missed. A simple "winter prep" checklist task per hive means nothing slips through before the cold sets in. See [Tasks](/using-the-app/tasks).

## Winter: the cluster rests

In cold weather the bees form a cluster, vibrating to keep warm and slowly working through their stores. There is little for you to do, and opening the hive chills the cluster and wastes precious food.

What to do:

- Leave them alone. Resist the urge to open up.
- Heft hives occasionally to judge remaining stores. In a hard, late winter a colony can starve within centimetres of food it cannot reach in the cold.
- If needed, give emergency solid food (fondant) placed directly over the cluster, with the briefest possible disturbance.
- Keep entrances clear of dead bees, snow and debris.
- Use the quiet months to maintain equipment, read, and review your records for the year just gone.

:::tip Review and plan
Winter is the perfect time to look back. Reviewing each hive's inspections, harvests and treatments shows which colonies thrived and what to change. Carry the lessons into next spring's plan.
:::

## Bringing it together

The year is a loop: build-up, swarm season, honey flow, treat and feed, prepare, rest. Each phase sets up the next, and good records are what connect them.

The more consistently you record inspections, tasks, harvests and treatments, the easier the following year becomes. For more on the colony's biology behind these rhythms, see [The colony](/beekeeping/the-colony), and for the wider beekeeping guides start at the [Beekeeping section](/category/beekeeping-basics).
